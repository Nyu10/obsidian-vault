Chapter 2


Book II of Saint Augustine’s _Confessions_ is one of the most psychologically intense and philosophically foundational sections of the entire work. It chronicles his sixteenth year (a gap year of enforced idleness due to his family's financial situation) spent in his hometown of Thagaste.

Rather than a dry historical recount, Augustine uses this chapter to dissect the anatomy of human sin, the psychology of peer pressure, the nature of perverted desires, and the famous theft of the pears.

## 1. The Theological Purpose of Remembering Sin

Augustine begins Book II by addressing a common paradox: why should a holy man, now a bishop, spend so much time vividly recounting his past sexual depravity and sins? His answer is that examining his past brokenness is not an act of masochism or hidden nostalgia, but a direct catalyst for praising God's mercy.

> "I will now call to mind my past foulness, and the carnal corruptions of my soul; not because I love them, but that I may love Thee, O my God. For love of Thy love I do it; reviewing my most wicked ways in the very bitterness of my remembrance, that Thou mayest grow sweet unto me (Thou sweetness never failing, Thou blissful and assured sweetness); and gathering me again out of that my dissipation, wherein I was torn piecemeal, while turned from Thee, the One, I lost myself among many vanities." (II.1)

### Key Concept: Dissipation vs. Unity

Augustine introduces a core philosophical theme here: sin is **dissipation** (a scattering or tearing apart of the self into a "multitude" of distractions), while righteousness is **unity** (re-collecting oneself in the One true God). When a person turns away from God, their soul fragments.

## 2. Adolescent Lust and the Distortion of Love

Augustine spends the first few sections of Book II mapping out the overwhelming onset of puberty and sexual desire during his sixteenth year. He does not blame the physical body itself, but rather his inability to distinguish between the holy, orderly beauty of friendship and the chaotic, blinding fog of lust.

> "But what was it that I delighted in save to love and to be beloved? But I held it not in moderation, mind to mind, the bright path of friendship, but out of the dark concupiscence of the flesh and the effervescence of youth exhalations came forth which obscured and overcast my heart, so that I was unable to discern pure affection from unholy desire. Both boiled confusedly within me, and dragged away my unstable youth into the rough places of unchaste desires, and plunged me into a gulf of infamy." (II.2)

### The Divine "Silence" and Internal Scourges

As Augustine fell deeper into sexual sin, he felt that God was silent. However, upon reflection, he realizes God was actually whispering through the natural psychological consequences and "bitter alloys" that accompany empty pleasures.

> "I was tossed to and fro, and wasted, and poured out, and boiled over in my fornications, and Thou heldest Thy peace, O Thou my tardy joy! Thou then heldest Thy peace, and I wandered further and further from Thee, into more and more fruitless seed-plots of sorrows, with a proud dejectedness, and a restless weariness... For Thou wert ever with me mercifully rigorous, and besprinkling with most bitter alloy all my unlawful pleasures: that I might seek pleasures without alloy." (II.2)

## 3. The Secular Ambitions of His Father vs. The Holy Fear of His Mother

During this sixteenth year, Augustine was forced to stay home from school in Carthage because his father, Patricius, was saving up money to send him back. Augustine contrasts the priorities of his pagan father with those of his devout Christian mother, Monica.

### Patricius: The Pride of Speech

Patricius was a man of modest means who went into debt to provide his son with a high-class classical education. Yet, Augustine notes with bitter irony that his father cared deeply about his eloquence but cared nothing for his soul.

> "Who did not extol my father, for that beyond the ability of his means, he would furnish his son with all necessaries for a far journey for his studies' sake? For many far abler citizens did no such thing for their children. But yet this same father had no concern how I grew towards Thee, or how chaste I were; so that I were but copious in speech, however barren I were to Thy culture, O God, who art the only true and good Lord of Thy field, my heart. But while in that my sixteenth year I lived with my parents, leaving all school for a while... the briers of unclean desires grew rank over my head, and there was no hand to root them out." (II.3)

### Monica: The Holy Warning

When Patricius noticed Augustine hitting physical maturity at the public baths, he gleefully looked forward to grandchildren. Monica, however, was terrified of the spiritual danger her son was in.

> "In my mother's breast Thou hadst already begun to build Thy temple and the foundation of Thy holy habitation... She therefore was startled with a holy fear and trembling; for though I had not yet been baptized, she feared those crooked ways in which they walk who turn their backs to Thee and not their faces... She advised me to chastity... but I rushed on headlong with such blindness that, among my friends, I was ashamed to be less shameless than they, when I heard them boasting of their disgraceful exploits... and I made myself out worse than I was, in order that I might not go lacking for praise." (II.3)

## 4. The Anatomy of Sin: The Pear Theft

The final and most famous movement of Book II is the theft of the pears. Augustine and a gang of unruly teenagers stole a massive amount of fruit from a neighbor's tree late at night. They didn't even want to eat the pears; they ended up throwing them to the pigs.

```
                  [ TRADITIONAL SIN ]
       Appetite/Desire ---> Objects (Food, Wealth, Sex)
       
                  [ AUGUSTINE'S PEAR THEFT ]
       No Desire For Fruit ---> Pure Malice / Loving the Sin Itself
```

Augustine uses this petty crime to interrogate the nature of **pure evil**. Normally, humans sin to gain something beautiful or useful (money, safety, pleasure, status). But Augustine stole simply because it was forbidden.

> "A pear tree there was near our vineyard, laden with fruit, tempting neither for colour nor taste. To shake and rob this, some lewd young fellows of us went, late one night... and took huge loads, not for our eating, but to fling to the very hogs, having only tasted them; and this, but to do what we liked only because it was misliked. Behold my heart, O God, behold my heart, which Thou hadst pity upon in the bottom of the bottomless pit. Now, behold, let my heart tell Thee what it sought there, that I should be gratuitously evil, having no temptation to ill, but the ill itself. It was foul, and I loved it; I loved to perish, I loved mine own fault, not that for which I was faulty, but my fault itself." (II.4)

### The Perverted Mimicry of God

Why do humans do evil for the sake of evil? Augustine concludes that sin is an unholy, distorted attempt to mimic God. By breaking a law with impunity, the sinner tries to experience a false sense of absolute freedom and omnipotence.

> "What then did I love in that theft? And wherein did I even corruptly and pervertedly imitate my Lord? Did I wish even by deceit to make a show of liberty, pretending a thumb-cut power, while by doing what was unpermitted I achieved a darkened likeness of omnipotence? Behold, that servant fleeing from his master, and catching a fleeting shadow of liberty! What a dejectedness, what a mountain of pride, what a restless weariness!" (II.6)

## 5. The Psychology of Peer Pressure: "Alone I Would Not Have Done It"

Finally, Augustine isolates the social element of the crime. If he had been alone, he never would have stolen the pears. He reveals the dangerous human instinct to conform to a group, where individuals lose their moral compass just to avoid the shame of being thought virtuous.

> "O friendship too unfriendly! Thou incomprehensible seducer of the soul, thou greediness to do mischief out of mirth and wantonness, thou thirst of others' loss, without any desire of my own gain or revenge! But because a voice says, ‘Let us go, let us do it,’ we are ashamed not to be shameless... I would not then have done it alone; no, altogether alone I would not have done it. Behold the setting of my heart before Thee, O my God. Alone I would not have committed that theft, wherein what I loved was not that which I stole, but that I stole it; which alone I would not have loved to do, nor alone have done it." (II.8-9)

Augustine ends Book II where he began: reflecting on his internal fragmentation. By running away from the steady, peaceful unity of God and chasing the chaotic acceptance of his peers, he became a wasteland to himself.





Chapter 3

Book III of _The Confessions_ marks a major transitional phase in Augustine's life. Now seventeen, he leaves his small hometown of Thagaste and arrives in the bustling, cosmopolitan metropolis of Carthage to pursue his higher education in rhetoric.

In this book, Augustine explores the seductive dangers of high culture—specifically the theater, academic vanity, and his fateful nine-year entrapment within the Manichaean heresy. Ultimately, Book III centers on a profound paradox: his intellectual search for truth actually led him further into spiritual blindness.

## 1. Carthage, Lust, and the Seduction of the Theater

Augustine famously opens Book III with a line that captures the overwhelming sensory and sexual overload of his new environment. Carthage was a city rife with pagan luxury and romantic intrigue, and his internal desires instantly matched the external environment.

> "To Carthage I came, where there sang all around me in my ears a cauldron of unholy loves. I loved not yet, yet I loved to love, and by a secret want, I hated myself for wanting less. I sought what I might love, in love with loving, and safety I hated, and a way without snares... For within me was a famine of that inward food, Thyself, my God; yet, through that famine, I was not hungry; but was without all longing for incorruptible sustenance, not because I was filled therewith, but the more empty I was, the more I loathed it." (III.1)

### The Illusion of Romantic and Theatrical Tragedy

To satisfy this "love of loving," Augustine frequented the Carthage theaters. He notes the strange psychological phenomenon where humans find joy in feeling sorrow for fictional characters, contrasting it with the true, healing sorrow of Christian repentance.

> "Stage-plays also carried me away, full of images of my miseries, and of fuel to my fire. Why is it, that man desires to be made sad, beholding doleful and tragical things, which yet himself would by no means suffer? Yet he desires as a spectator to feel sorrow at them, and this very sorrow is his pleasure. What is this but a miserable madness? For a man is the more affected with these actions, the more he himself is not free from such passions; howsoever, when he suffers in his own person, it is styled misery; when he compassionates others, then it is mercy. But what sort of compassion is this for feigned and theatrical scenes?" (III.2)

## 2. Academic Pride and the Subversives (_Eversores_)

As a student of rhetoric, Augustine excelled rapidly. However, classical education in the Roman Empire was highly competitive and fueled by vanity. He aimed to become a top lawyer or state official, seeking human applause rather than divine approval.

He explicitly distances himself from a toxic clique of older students known as the "Subversives" (_Eversores_), who used bullying and psychological disruption to destabilize newer students.

> "And now I was chief in the rhetoric school, whereat I joyed proudly, and swelled with arrogancy, though more modest than they, O Lord, Thou knowest, and altogether a stranger to the subverting acts of the 'Subversives' (for this cruel and devilish name was held to be the very badge of gallantry) among whom I lived, with a shameless shamefacedness, because I was not such as they... Yet I lived among them, and sometimes delighted in their friendship, whose actions I ever abhorred—that is, their subvertings, wherewith they wantonly insulted the modesty of strangers, which they disturbed by unprovoked jeers, feeding their own malicious mirth." (III.3)

## 3. The Turning Point: Cicero’s _Hortensius_

In his nineteenth year, following the standard curriculum, Augustine read a now-lost philosophical dialogue by the Roman orator Cicero called _Hortensius_. This book changed the entire trajectory of his life. Instead of teaching him _how_ to speak (rhetoric), it ignited a burning passion for _what_ to seek—Wisdom itself.

```
              [ CLASSICAL RHETORIC ] -> Focused on form, style, and winning arguments.
                         │
               ( Augustine reads Hortensius )
                         ▼
               [ TRUE PHILOSOPHY ]    -> Focused on seeking eternal, objective Wisdom.
```

> "In the ordinary course of study, I fell upon a certain book of Cicero, whose speech almost all admire, not so his heart. This book of his contains an exhortation to philosophy, and is called 'Hortensius.' But this book quite altered my affection, and turned my prayers to Thyself, O Lord; and made me have other purposes and desires. Every vain hope at once became worthless to me, and I longed with an incredible burning desire for an immortality of wisdom, and I began to arise, that I might return to Thee. For not to sharpen my tongue did I employ that book... nor did it impress me by its way of speaking, but by what it spoke." (III.4)

### The Missing Name of Christ

Though Cicero awakened his intellect, Augustine felt a lingering void. Having been raised by Monica, the name of Jesus Christ was woven into his subconscious, and no book entirely devoid of that name could fully satisfy his soul.

> "For this name, according to Thy mercy, O Lord, this name of my Saviour, Thy Son, had my tender heart, even with my mother's milk, devoutly drunk in, and deeply treasured; and whatsoever was without that name, though never so learned, polished, or true, took not entire hold of me." (III.4)

## 4. Turning to Scripture and the Trap of the Manichees

Seeking to bridge his new love for wisdom with his childhood respect for Christ, Augustine turned to the Christian Scriptures. However, coming directly from the polished, majestic prose of Cicero, he found the humble, unrefined style of the Latin translations of the Bible offensive to his intellectual pride.

> "I resolved then to bend my mind to the holy Scriptures, that I might see what they were. And lo, I see a thing not understood by the proud, nor laid open to children, but lowly in access, in its recesses lofty, and veiled with mysteries; and I was not such as could enter into it, or stoop my neck to follow its steps. For when I then turned to those Scriptures, they appeared to me unworthy to be compared with the stateliness of Tully [Cicero]; for my swelling pride shrunk from their lowliness, and my sharp wit pierced not the interior thereof." (III.5)

### Falling into the Manichaean Heresy

Because his pride blinded him to the depth of Scripture, he fell prey to the Manichees—a dualistic, gnostic sect that claimed to offer a purely rational, scientific version of Christianity stripped of old superstitions. They mocked orthodox Christianity and seduced Augustine with empty, complex cosmic mythologies.

> "Therefore I fell among men proudly doting, exceeding carnal and prating, in whose mouths were the snares of the Devil, limed with the mixture of the syllables of Thy name, and of our Lord Jesus Christ, and of the Holy Ghost, the Comforter... They cried out 'Truth, Truth,' and spoke much thereof to me, yet it was not in them: but they spoke falsely, not only of Thee, who art truly the Truth, but even of those elements of this world, Thy creatures... O Truth, Truth, how inwardly did even then the marrow of my soul pant after Thee, when they often and diversely, and in many and huge books, echoed of Thee to me, though it was but an echo!" (III.6)

## 5. Theological Confusion: The Nature of God and Old Testament Morality

As a Manichee, Augustine struggled immensely with two major intellectual hurdles that orthodox Christians seemed unable to answer for him at the time: the problem of evil (if God is good, where does evil come from?) and the apparent immorality of Old Testament patriarchs (who practiced polygamy and animal sacrifice).

He later realized he was asking the wrong questions because he was thinking of God as a physical, material object extended in space.

> "For I knew not that God is a Spirit, not one who hath parts extended in length and breadth, or who hath bulk... And what should be in us, according to which we are like to God, and in Scripture are rightly said to be 'after the image of God.' Nor knew I that true inward righteousness, which judgeth not according to custom, but according to the most righteous law of God Almighty... whereby the ways of places and times were disposed, according to those times and places; itself mean time being the same always and everywhere, not one thing in one place, and another in another; according to which Abraham, and Isaac, and Jacob, and Moses, and David, and all those praised by the mouth of God, were righteous." (III.7)

## 6. Monica’s Tears and the Vision of the Ruler

The book concludes back in Thagaste, where Monica is devastated by her son's lapse into heresy. She initially refuses to let him live under her roof or eat at her table. However, God consoles her through a prophetic dream.

She sees herself standing on a wooden ruler, weeping, when a shining youth approaches and tells her that where she is standing, her son is standing too.

> "Thou sentest Thine hand from above, and drewest my soul out of that profound darkness, my mother, Thy faithful one, weeping to Thee for me, more than mothers weep the bodily deaths of their children. For she, by that faith and spirit which she had from Thee, discerned the death wherein I lay, and Thou heardest her, O Lord... For whence was that dream whereby Thou comfortedst her, so that she allowed me to live with her, and to eat at the same table in the house, which she had begun to shrink from, abhorring and detesting the blasphemies of my error? For she saw herself standing on a certain wooden rule, and a shining youth coming towards her, cheerful and smiling upon her, herself being sad, and overwhelmed with grief." (III.11)

### "The Son of These Tears Shall Not Perish"

Monica sought out a local bishop, begging him to debate Augustine and correct his errors. The bishop, wise to Augustine’s intellectual stubbornness, refused, saying the young man was still too enamored with the novelty of the heresy to listen. When Monica persisted with tears, the bishop gave her a famous, comforting dismissal that sustained her hope for years.

> "Go thy way, and God bless thee, for it is not possible that the son of these tears should perish. Which answer she took (as she often remembered in our conversations) as if it had sounded from heaven." (III.12)



These two chapters are essentially a masterclass in the psychology of human nature, guilt, and intellectual pride. If you need to speak intelligently about Books II and III of _The Confessions_ without having trudged through the text yourself, here is a breakdown of the most mind-blowing, insightful concepts Augustine introduces.

## 💡 Mind-Blowing Insights from Book II (The Anatomy of Sin)

### 1. The Psychology of Doing Evil _Just Because_ It’s Wrong (The Pear Theft)

- **The Insight:** Most people assume we commit crimes or do bad things for a tangible reward (money, power, lust, survival). Augustine blows this up by examining why he stole pears he didn't even want.
    
- **What's Crazy About It:** He introduces the terrifying concept of **gratuitous evil**—doing something wrong simply because it is forbidden. The thrill wasn't the fruit; the thrill was the pure malice of breaking a boundary. It’s the ancient equivalent of modern vandalism or trolling online just to cause chaos.
    

### 2. Sin as a "Perverted Mimicry" of God

- **The Insight:** When humans sin, we aren't actually creating new evils; we are clumsily trying to copy God’s attributes in a twisted way.
    
- **What's Crazy About It:** Augustine argues that when he stole, he was trying to experience a "darkened likeness of omnipotence." By breaking a rule and escaping punishment, a human is trying to pretend they are completely free and all-powerful, just like God. Every sin is just a counterfeit version of a divine virtue (e.g., pride mimics high-mindedness; lust mimics love).
    

### 3. The Mob Mentality: "Alone I Would Not Have Done It"

- **The Insight:** Augustine isolates the exact moment an individual's moral compass dissolves inside a group.
    
- **What's Crazy About It:** He admits that if he had been alone, he would have walked right past that pear tree. But the moment a group of peers says, _"Let's go, let's do it,"_ an intense psychological pressure kicks in. Augustine notes a bizarre double-shame: **we become ashamed not to be shameless.** We brag about doing worse things than we actually did just so our friends won't think we are innocent or weak.
    

### 4. Sin From a Philosophical Lens: Dissipation vs. Unity

- **The Insight:** Augustine doesn't view sin merely as breaking a legal rule; he views it as a state of psychological fragmentation.
    
- **What's Crazy About It:** He frames God as absolute **Unity** and peace. When you align with God, your mind and soul are gathered together. When you sin and pursue "many vanities" (pleasure, fame, peer approval), your soul undergoes **dissipation**—it literally gets torn piecemeal and scattered. Sin makes you lose yourself by dividing your attention among a thousand fleeting things.
    

## 💡 Mind-Blowing Insights from Book III (The Traps of the Intellect)

### 5. "In Love with Loving" (The Trap of Romanticism)

- **The Insight:** When Augustine moves to the big city (Carthage), he realizes he is suffering from an abstract, directionless emotional hunger. He wasn't in love with a specific person; he was "in love with the _idea_ of being in love."
    
- **What's Crazy About It:** He diagnoses a profound human condition: we often use others just to satisfy our own desire to feel passion. He notes that he actually _wanted_ to find someone or something to break his peace, deliberately seeking out emotional chaos and romantic snares because stability felt boring.
    

### 6. The Voyeurism of Tragedy (Why We Love Sad Movies/Plays)

- **The Insight:** Augustine visits theaters and realizes humans have a strange, messed-up appetite for watching fictional suffering.
    
- **What's Crazy About It:** He asks a brilliant psychological question: _Why do we pay money to be made sad by stories, when we would hate to experience that same tragedy in real life?_ He concludes that theatrical sorrow is a cheap, counterfeit form of mercy. It allows us to feel the rush of "compassion" or emotion without any of the real-world responsibility or sacrifice that actual empathy requires.
    

### 7. Intellectual Pride Blinds You to the Truth

- **The Insight:** Augustine reads a philosophy book (_Hortensius_) that genuinely inspires him to seek wisdom. But when he tries to read the Bible, he thinks it's trash because the writing style isn't as poetic or fancy as Cicero.
    
- **What's Crazy About It:** He highlights how being "smart" can actually make you stupid. His academic arrogance and "sharp wit" made him look down on the simplicity of the scriptures. Because he wanted to look intellectual, he fell for a weird, trendy cult (the Manichees) who promised "scientific and rational answers" but were actually just selling complex, pseudo-spiritual nonsense.
    

### 8. God is Extended in Spirit, Not Space

- **The Insight:** During his time in the cult, Augustine couldn't understand orthodox Christianity because he was trying to imagine God as a physical, material body.
    
- **What's Crazy About It:** He realizes that our human minds struggle to conceptualize anything that isn't physical. He was imagining God as a massive glowing light or a cosmic entity stretching across the universe. Book III sets up his massive breakthrough: realizing that God is a non-material Spirit, and that the "image of God" inside humans is found in our minds and souls, not our physical bodies.
    

## 🎯 The Ultimate Takeaway Checklist for Sharing

If you need to summarize these two chapters in a single conversation, hit these three points:

1. **The Pear Tree:** Human beings don't just sin for rewards; sometimes we do bad things strictly for the dark thrill of breaking a rule, especially when we are running with a crowd.
    
2. **The Counterfeit Life:** Everything we chase outside of God (sex, fame, theatrical drama, intellectual snobbery) is just a cheap, distorted copy of a deeper spiritual reality we are actually starving for.
    
3. **The Parent Trap:** Augustine's father cared immensely about his son's _career and eloquence_ but ignored his morality; his mother cared entirely about his _soul_ and wept for his choices—proving that cultural success and spiritual health are often completely at odds.
