- **Deep Research:** Pick one sector (like data centers, lithium mining, or specialized infrastructure) and write a short "white paper" for yourself on why it’s undervalued or why it fits your long-term view.
    
- **Track Your "System":** Use a simple spreadsheet to track why you bought something, your "thesis" (the reason you bought it), and your exit criteria. This turns investing into a data-driven process rather than just guessing.
    
- **Backtesting/Modeling:** Use your skills to look at historical data. How did those commodities perform during the 2008 crash? How did REITs hold up when interest rates were at 5%? Finding the answers to these questions is far more engaging than just watching a ticker move.

Investment house hacking

> I am an infrastructure engineer currently building a liquidity position for a house-hack investment in Ann Arbor, with an intended purchase timeline within the next 12 months.
> 
> I’m not looking for a traditional residential buyer’s agent; I’m looking for an investment-focused partner who understands the local rental market, cap rates, and zoning/ADU potential in [Neighborhoods, e.g., Kerrytown, Old West Side].
> 
> My goal is to secure a property that functions as a strong rental asset long-term. Do you have time for a 15-minute call to discuss your current take on the local multi-family inventory and what you look for when vetting potential house hacks for clients?"

### **The "Red Flag" Test**
When you get them on the phone, ask these three questions. If they struggle with these, they aren't the right partner for you:
- **"Do you or your partners personally own any investment properties in Ann Arbor?"** (If they don't, they don't have "skin in the game").
- **"Can you help me analyze a property’s Cash-on-Cash return, or should I bring my own pro-forma?"** (A pro investor agent will immediately talk about NOI, OpEx, and rent rolls).
- **"What’s the current 'rentability' of the [Specific Neighborhood] market for a 2-4 unit property?"**
