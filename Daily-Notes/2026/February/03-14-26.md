### 1. The "Criterion of Embarrassment"
This is a major rule in historical strikes. If you were making up "propaganda" to start a perfect religion, you wouldn't include details that make your leader look bad, weak, or confusing.
- **The Example:** In the Gospels, Jesus says he doesn’t know the timing of the end of the world—only the Father knows. If you’re inventing a God-man, you don't give him "limited knowledge."
- **The Logic:** Historians argue that since these "embarrassing" bits are there, the writers were likely recording a memory they felt they _had_ to include, even if it was awkward.
### 2. Semitic "Flavor" (Aramaic Undercurrents)
The Gospels were written in **Greek**, but when you translate many of Jesus’s sayings back into **Aramaic** (his actual language), they suddenly rhyme, have rhythm, or contain wordplays that only work in Aramaic.

- **The "Voiceprint":** It’s like reading a book in English that was clearly translated from French—the idioms and "flavor" give away the origin.
    
- **The Logic:** If the early Church (which was becoming mostly Greek-speaking) was just making things up, they wouldn't have preserved these specific, clunky Aramaic structures.
    

### 3. Distinctive Style (The "Amen")

Jesus had linguistic "tics" that were unique to him.

- **"Amen, I say to you":** In Jewish tradition, "Amen" was said _after_ someone else spoke to agree with them. Jesus is the only figure in ancient literature who puts "Amen" at the _beginning_ of his own sentences to mean "Truly/Listen up."
    
- **The Logic:** This is a "voiceprint." It’s so unique and consistent across different sources (Mark, Matthew, Luke, and John) that it’s highly likely it traces back to the actual man.
