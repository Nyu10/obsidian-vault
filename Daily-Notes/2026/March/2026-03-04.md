Today, we had a "Black Mirror" moment in the middle of a Greek postcard.

We wandered into a tiny place—Τσιπουράδικο του Καραγιάννη—completely by accident. We were actually hunting for the restaurant right next door, lured by the promise of pistachio pesto pasta. But in the confusing layout of the street, we stepped through the wrong door, and suddenly we were in another world entirely.

It was quiet, still, and presided over by a "Grandpa" figure who didn’t speak a word of English. He looked tired—a deep, generational tiredness. Before ordering, I did what we all do now: I checked the digital pulse of the place. The internet had already decided his fate. A 2.0 rating. A string of 1-star reviews from only the last 11 months, all warning of "bad seafood" and "expensive" bills.

The guilt hit me instantly. I pulled up a translation on my phone to explain: "Sorry, we didn't find anything we like, can we just pay for the water?"

He looked at the screen, half-understanding the AI-translated Greek, but he wasn't ready to let the table go yet. He kept pointing to the rest of the menu, double-checking, insisting the fish was good, trying one last time to make a connection. When he realized we really weren't staying, he was going to charge us 2 euros for the water. I reached for my credit card, but then I remembered the reviews: They mostly only accept cash. When he saw the card, he didn't even try. He just sighed, took the water back, and walked away. He seemed so sad—not angry, just defeated by a world of digital translations and plastic cards that he doesn't quite fit into anymore.

The worst part? Once we were back on the sidewalk, we couldn't even bring ourselves to go to the place next door for the pistachio pasta we actually wanted. We didn't want him to see us through the window, sitting comfortably at the "successful" restaurant right next to his empty tables. We just kept walking.

We didn't stay, but we didn't just walk away either. We left a review—not because the experience was perfect, but because he’s a human being who shouldn't be erased by a star rating. We bumped him from a 2.0 to a 2.2. A tiny digital "life jacket" in a sea of 1-star warnings.

I still feel a bit heavy. You don’t just see the ruins in Greece; sometimes you see the people living in the shadows of the modern world, trying to keep the lights on for one more night while the world—and the pistachio pesto—moves on without them
